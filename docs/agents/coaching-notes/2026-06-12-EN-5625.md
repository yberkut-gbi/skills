# Coaching note — EN-5625: pm-discover + pm-frame

## What went well

**The ADR was the right starting point.** Reading ADR 0001 §3 (skill placement) and §8 (PM conductors) before writing a single line made the scope immediately clear: two judgment-heavy skills, product group, opus model, project-agnostic, feeding the conductor pipeline. No alignment stage was needed — the ADR had already locked the design.

**facilitation.md integration was clean.** Both skills cite the patterns (AFCI for artifacts-first discipline in pm-discover; PDF-Loop for solution-bets presentation in pm-frame) without making them mandatory. The "cite as reference, not enforced" framing from ADR §9 translated naturally into a short facilitation section in each skill.

**The pipeline seam is explicit.** Each skill names its upstream and downstream explicitly (pm-discover → pm-frame → core-grill → pm-to-prd; conductor skills at the bottom). A reader of either skill can trace where it fits in the flow without hunting the README.

## What to watch next cycle

**pm-frame's solution-bets step is the most judgment-intensive part.** When pm-frame runs inside pm-discover-flow or pm-flow, the conductor will need to handle the case where the human rejects all three bets and wants to reframe — the skill as written hands off cleanly after confirmation, but the conductor needs a re-entry path. Keep this in mind when writing EN-5627 (pm-discover-flow).

**Standalone vs. conductor mode.** Both skills mention they can run standalone, but the experience diverges from conductor mode (no resume-by-artifact, no cost record). This is fine for now — S9-S11 will define the conductor protocol. Just don't assume standalone and conductor produce the same artifacts.

## Efficiency note

This was a low-cost cycle: no implementation code, no tests, no green gate. Two SKILL.md files and a README update. The ticket was well-specified (AC matched exactly what the ADR said). Sub-agents were not needed. The main time was in reading the ADR and facilitation.md carefully before writing — time well spent to avoid re-reading mid-draft.
